The case for taking pre-requisite knowledge seriously (but fearlessly)â€‹

When it comes to mathematics (and quantum mechanics has a lot of it), it is common currency to hold the belief that some gifted people simply "get it" faster than others. But does this belief actually hold true to reality? For the most part, at least, it seems that background is the real dealbraker.

Understand the pre-requisites

Quantum mechanics is not really that hard; it just has a lot of pre-requisite knowledge. If the pre-requisite knowledge is practiced and learned at the right pace at the right time, anyone can understand, master, and apply the theory.

Do not overlook the pre-requisites

The same is true for the opposite scenario: without the right pre-requisite knowledge, the most seemingly straightforward lecture on quantum mechanics will be as strange as an unfamiliar alphabet and as hard as trying to read it. But to blame is not the lack of talent, but the pedagogy (whether self-taught or not).

Physics backgroundâ€‹

Contrary to the way that quantum mechanics is usually taught, it is possible to understand the theory without most of the math. The mathematical formulation of the theory is not the same as the theory, but a representation of it. With enough words and good prose, it should be possible explain quantum mechanics with verbal logic.

It is also a good idea explain the history of the subject. By understanding how it came about, we gain a lot of insight into the theory that we don't get if we start with the modern mathematical formulation.

What is a quantum system?

What are examples of quantum systems?
  • If you are interested in how a hydrogen atom behaves, then your quantum system is that hydrogen atom (in addition to any extra necessary information such as the position and what's happening in its surroundings).
  • If you place the hydrogen atom in a magnetic field, then your quantum system is that hydrogen atom in the magnetic field.
  • If you look deeper at the hydrogen atom, the subatomic particles such as the electron, neutron, proton, ane even photon may constitute your quantum system.
What is a quantum state?

A quantum state is the mathematical description of our quantum system, i.e., it's an expression that "stores" as much information as we know about the quantum system.

Indeed, the quantum state object holds everything about the state of the quantum system!

First Postulate of Quantum Mechanics

The state of a physical system is characterized by a state vector that belongs to a complex vector space V\mathbf{V}V, called the state space of the system.

The first postulate from quantum mechanics encourages you to get a refresher on the basic properties of vector spaces from linear algebra.

Second Postulate of Quantum Mechanics

A physical quantity A\mathcal{A}A is described by an operator A^\mathcal{\hat{A}}A^ acting on the state space V\mathcal{V}V, and this operator is an observable.

Operators are the mathematical objects that allow us to describe physical quantities in quantum mechanics.
