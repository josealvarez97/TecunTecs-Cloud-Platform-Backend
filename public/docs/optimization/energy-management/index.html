Energy Management

Power flows in a static networkâ€‹

Model descriptionâ€‹

Moehle et al. (2019) present a mathematical model for a spatial distribution of generators and loads in a network. The basic idea is that you may want to plan the operation of the generators, but only after considering how the power flows through the network into the loads.

Insert graphic here

Capturing the idea of spatial phenomena is a very good idea: You may want to consider how the price of power can vary at different locations on a network, e.g., power is typically cheaper close to cheap generators, and more expensive close to loads (transmission may be sometimes difficult) (Moehle et al., 2019).

So, consider power flows across a network that are constant over time (in real life, imagine you are working over an interval such as one hour). This necessarily implies that any power can be converted to energy by multiplying it by time.

According to Moehle et al. (2019), the model consists of three abstractions:

  1. Devices: Produce, consume, and transfer power.
  2. Terminals: Each device has one or more terminals. Power can flow in either direction in such terminals.
  3. Nets: Exchange power between terminals.

Altogether, the three abstractions constitute the (static) network model, a collection of devices and nets that connects each terminal of each device to one of the nets.

There are important properties associated to the model by Moehle et al. (2019):

  • Total cost: The sum of the costs of all devices.
  • Feasibility: The set of terminal powers is feasible if the cost is finite and if power conservation holds at each net.
  • Optimality: The set of terminal powers is optimal if it is not only feasible but also minimizes the total cost.
